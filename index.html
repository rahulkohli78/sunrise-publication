Project Sunrise Analysis

What Is the Price
of Speed?

Range was solved years ago. Pricing wasn't.

What remains is whether the airline can charge enough to cover what a nonstop service actually costs to run, without asking more than travellers have already shown they will pay for the time it saves.

Comparable routes, Perth–London, Newark–Singapore, and today's one-stop Sydney–London, suggest travellers have paid roughly 18 to 22 percent more than a one-stop fare for a comparable time saving.

The gap is narrow. Project Sunrise does not sit comfortably inside what comparable markets have already demonstrated travellers will pay. It sits just beyond it, close enough to be credible, not close enough to be proven.

Close enough to be credible. Not close enough to be proven.
The evidence

Across 5,000 simulated outcomes per route, varying fuel price and load factor, the required premium stayed below 25 percent in roughly six runs out of ten on each route. The worst one-in-twenty runs pushed it to around 36 percent, well beyond the range observed on comparable routes.

Behind the diagnostic

The analysis rests on four choices.

01 Why use a range rather than one estimate?Why one break-even calculation is not enough.

Each route was modelled 5,000 times, varying fuel price and load factor in every run. 22.5 and 23.1 percent are the break-even premiums under the model's anchor assumptions. The median across the full simulated distribution lands close by, at 22.3 and 23.3 percent, confirming the anchor case sits near the centre of the distribution rather than at an extreme.

“The decision is not whether one estimate works. It is how often the economics remain inside an acceptable range.”
02 Why use aircraft-specific costs?Why a fleet average would understate the mission.

Project Sunrise is not a standard long-haul mission. Additional crew, crew-rest facilities, fewer seats and extended maintenance cycles change the cost per seat. A fleet-average cost would understate what the route is actually asking the market to support.

“A route-specific decision requires a route-specific cost base.”
03 Why use comparable nonstop and near-nonstop routes?Why observed fares are stronger than inferred willingness to pay.

Perth–London and Newark–Singapore provide observed evidence of what travellers have paid for a materially faster nonstop journey over a one-stop alternative. Today's Sydney–London one-stop premium adds a third, more directly comparable data point.

“The aircraft defines what is possible. Comparable markets define what has already been paid for.”
04 Why separate cost recovery from willingness to pay?Why the model cannot prove market acceptance.

The model estimates what the airline needs to charge. It cannot establish that travellers will pay that amount, or continue paying it once the novelty of a nonstop service fades.

“Cost recovery is a modelling question. Market acceptance is a behavioural one.”
Decision boundaries
The model identifies the fare required to recover cost. It does not establish that people will pay it.

The model cannot tell us:

  • How demand responds as the fare gap widens
  • Whether the premium survives once the novelty fades
  • How premium-cabin demand behaves in weaker years
  • Whether the airline gains share across the corridor or simply fills one aircraft
  • How competitors respond once a nonstop product exists
The next analytical question

What happens to demand once the nonstop premium is no longer new? Does it hold, or does it need proving all over again?

Every field eventually reaches a point where the cost of reach exceeds what people are willing to pay for the time it saves. Aviation shows that point in block hours and ticket prices. Project Sunrise makes the boundary visible.
The aircraft can clear the distance. The market still has to clear the price.
A note on current conditions

July 2026. This analysis was built in November 2025, when jet fuel was priced at US$0.80 a kilogram. That price is the anchor behind every figure above, and the six sections above are a fixed record of that analysis.

Fuel has since moved. At today's global benchmark, roughly US$0.92 a kilogram, rerunning the identical model puts the required premium at 27.1 percent on Sydney–New York and 27.8 percent on Sydney–London. Nothing else in the model changed; the shift is fuel alone.
